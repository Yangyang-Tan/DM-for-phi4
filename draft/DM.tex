\section{Diffusion models}
\label{sec:dm_framework}

Diffusion models~\cite{Ho2020DDPM,Song2021SDE} are a class of generative models that learn a target distribution $p_{\text{data}}(\phi)$ by inverting a gradual noising process. In the forward direction, a data sample $\phi_0\sim p_{\text{data}}$ is progressively corrupted into pure noise $\phi_{t_{\max}}$ over a fictitious time interval $t\in[0,t_{\max}]$. In the reverse direction, the model learns to denoise, thereby transporting samples from an analytically tractable prior back to the data distribution. Unlike one-shot generators such as GANs or VAEs, diffusion models decompose the generative task into a sequence of local denoising problems, each individually tractable and composing into an expressive and stable sampler. This structure is especially well suited to lattice field configurations. High-quality training data can be produced by standard Monte Carlo, and because the action $S[\phi]$ is known in closed form, the generated samples can be exactness-corrected against $e^{-S[\phi]}$ via a Metropolis-adjusted Langevin (MALA) step driven by the learned score.

\subsection{Forward process as a stochastic differential equation}
\label{sec:dm_forward}

The forward noising process can be formulated as an It\^o stochastic differential equation~\cite{Song2021SDE}
\begin{equation}
\mathrm{d}\phi \;=\; f(\phi,t)\,\mathrm{d}t \;+\; g(t)\,\mathrm{d}w,
\label{eq:forward_sde}
\end{equation}
where $f(\phi,t)$ is the drift term, $g(t)$ the diffusion coefficient, and $w$ is a Wiener process with independent components at every spatial point. Equivalently, the Langevin form reads
\begin{equation}
\begin{aligned}
\frac{\partial \phi_t(x)}{\partial t} &\;=\; f\!\bigl(\phi_t(x),t\bigr) \;+\; g(t)\,\eta(t,x),\\
\bigl\langle \eta(t,x)\,\eta(t',x')\bigr\rangle &\;=\; \delta(x-x')\,\delta(t-t'),
\end{aligned}
\label{eq:forward_langevin}
\end{equation}
with $\eta$ a Gaussian white noise uncorrelated between distinct spatial points $x$. Two canonical choices of $(f,g)$ have emerged.

\paragraph{Variance-preserving(VP)}
The variance-preserving choice takes $f(\phi,t)=-\tfrac{1}{2}\beta(t)\phi$ and $g(t)=\sqrt{\beta(t)}$, with $\beta(t)>0$ controlling both the drift and the noise strength. The drift is proportional to $\phi$ with a negative coefficient, and hence acts as a restoring force towards zero. Integrating \Eq{eq:forward_sde} over the interval $[0,t]$ from the initial condition $\phi_0$, the distribution of $\phi_t$ conditioned on $\phi_0$ is a Gaussian,
\begin{equation}
p_{0t}\bigl(\phi_t\,\bigl|\,\phi_0\bigr) \;=\; \mathcal{N}\!\Bigl(\phi_t;\,\sqrt{\bar\alpha(t)}\,\phi_0,\,(1-\bar\alpha(t))\,\mathbb{1}\Bigr),
\label{eq:vp_kernel}
\end{equation}
with $\bar\alpha(t)=\exp\!\bigl(-\int_{0}^{t}\beta(s)\,\mathrm{d}s\bigr)$ decreasing from $1$ at $t=0$ to $0$ as $t\to\infty$. The conditional mean $\sqrt{\bar\alpha(t)}\,\phi_0$ shrinks from $\phi_0$ towards zero while the conditional variance $1-\bar\alpha(t)$ grows from $0$ to $1$, so the endpoint distribution approaches the standard Gaussian $\mathcal{N}(0,\mathbb{1})$ regardless of $\phi_0$. For unit-variance data, $\mathrm{Var}[\phi_t]=\bar\alpha(t)\,\mathrm{Var}[\phi_0]+(1-\bar\alpha(t))=1$ at all $t$, giving the schedule its name \textit{Variance-preserving}. \Eq{eq:vp_kernel} is also the continuous-time limit of the DDPM formulation of Ho~\emph{et al.}~\cite{Ho2020DDPM}.

\paragraph{Variance-exploding(VE).}
The variance-exploding choice takes $f(\phi,t)=0$ and a positive diffusion coefficient $g(t)$ as a prescribed noise schedule, so the SDE reads
\begin{equation}
\mathrm{d}\phi \;=\; g(t)\,\mathrm{d}w.
\label{eq:ve_sde_explicit}
\end{equation}
The absence of drift leaves the initial field $\phi_0$ untouched while noise of growing variance is injected on top, from which the schedule takes its name. Integrating \Eq{eq:ve_sde_explicit} over the interval $[0,t]$ from the initial condition $\phi_0$, the distribution of $\phi_t$ conditioned on $\phi_0$ is a Gaussian,
\begin{equation}
p_{0t}\bigl(\phi_t\,\bigl|\,\phi_0\bigr) \;=\; \mathcal{N}\!\bigl(\phi_t;\,\phi_0,\,\sigma^{2}(t)\,\mathbb{1}\bigr),
\label{eq:ve_sde_kernel}
\end{equation}
with conditional mean fixed at $\phi_0$ and conditional variance $\sigma^2(t)\,\mathbb{1}$, where $\sigma^2(t)=\int_0^t g(s)^2\,\mathrm{d}s$ is the accumulated noise variance and grows monotonically from $0$ at $t=0$. The terminal noise scale $\sigma_{\max}$ is typically chosen following the prescription of Song \& Ermon~\cite{SongErmon2020}, $\sigma_{\max}\gtrsim\max_{i,j}\|\phi_{i}-\phi_{j}\|_{2}$, where $\phi_{i}$, $\phi_{j}$ run over pairs of training configurations. This bound ensures that the noise-smeared distribution $p_{\sigma_{\max}}(\phi)=\int p_{\text{data}}(\phi')\,\mathcal{N}\!\bigl(\phi;\phi',\sigma_{\max}^2\mathbb{1}\bigr)\,\mathrm{d}\phi'$ is effectively a single Gaussian that retains essentially no information about the initial field $\phi_0$. This formulation corresponds to the NCSN/SMLD line of work~\cite{SongErmon2019}.

Throughout this work we adopt the VE form with the geometric schedule $g(t)=\sigma^{t}$ on $t\in[0,1]$, parametrised by a single scalar hyperparameter $\sigma>1$. For this choice the accumulated variance evaluates in closed form,
\begin{equation}
\sigma^{2}(t) \;=\; \int_{0}^{t}\!g(s)^{2}\,\mathrm{d}s \;=\; \frac{\sigma^{2t}-1}{2\ln\sigma},
\label{eq:ve_sigma_schedule}
\end{equation}
with endpoint $\sigma(1)=\sqrt{(\sigma^{2}-1)/(2\ln\sigma)}$ setting the maximum noise scale realised during training. Note that the hyperparameter $\sigma$ is \emph{not} the terminal noise scale $\sigma_{\max}$. The two are related by $\sigma_{\max}=\sigma(1)$.

\subsection{Reverse-time generation}
\label{sec:dm_reverse}

To draw samples from $p_{\text{data}}$ we must invert the forward dynamics of~\Eq{eq:forward_sde}, transporting an endpoint $\phi_{t_{\max}}\sim p_{t_{\max}}$ drawn from the tractable terminal distribution back to $t=0$. Anderson~\cite{Anderson1982} showed that the time-reversal of an It\^o SDE is itself an It\^o SDE. Applied to~\Eq{eq:forward_sde}, yields~\cite{Song2021SDE}
\begin{equation}
\mathrm{d}\phi \;=\; \bigl[f(\phi,t)-g^{2}(t)\,\nabla_{\phi}\log p_{t}(\phi)\bigr]\,\mathrm{d}t \;+\; g(t)\,\mathrm{d}\bar{w},
\label{eq:reverse_sde_exact}
\end{equation}
where~\Eq{eq:reverse_sde_exact} is understood as being integrated in the reverse direction from $t=t_{\max}$ down to $t=0$, so that $\mathrm{d}t<0$ is an infinitesimal negative time step, and $\mathrm{d}\bar{w}$ denotes an independent standard Wiener increment at each such step. Song~\emph{et al.}~\cite{Song2021SDE} further established the existence of a deterministic probability-flow ODE
\begin{equation}
\frac{\mathrm{d}\phi}{\mathrm{d}t} \;=\; f(\phi,t)-\tfrac{1}{2}\,g^{2}(t)\,\nabla_{\phi}\log p_{t}(\phi)
\label{eq:prob_flow_ode_exact}
\end{equation}
whose marginal density at every $t\in[0,t_{\max}]$ coincides with that of the forward SDE~\Eq{eq:forward_sde} and the reverse SDE~\Eq{eq:reverse_sde_exact}. Concretely, an ensemble initialised at $\phi_{0}\sim p_{\text{data}}$ and evolved forward in $t$ under~\Eq{eq:forward_sde}, or initialised at $\phi_{t_{\max}}\sim p_{t_{\max}}$ and evolved backward in $t$ under either~\Eq{eq:reverse_sde_exact} or~\Eq{eq:prob_flow_ode_exact}, produces the same marginal $p_{t}(\phi)$ at every intermediate time slice, even though the three processes generate distinct individual trajectories. Being deterministic,~\Eq{eq:prob_flow_ode_exact} admits integration in either direction of $t$. For generative sampling we integrate it in the reverse direction from $t=t_{\max}$ down to $t=0$, starting from $\phi_{t_{\max}}\sim p_{t_{\max}}$.

The crucial observation is that both reverse dynamics depend on the data distribution only through the time-dependent \emph{score function}
\begin{equation}
s(\phi,t) \;=\; \nabla_{\phi}\log p_{t}(\phi),
\label{eq:score_def}
\end{equation}
a vector of the same dimension as $\phi$. Replacing $s(\phi,t)$ in~\Eq{eq:reverse_sde_exact} and~\Eq{eq:prob_flow_ode_exact} with a neural approximation $s_{\theta}(\phi,t)$ yields the practical samplers
\begin{align}
\mathrm{d}\phi &\;=\; \bigl[f(\phi,t)-g^{2}(t)\,s_{\theta}(\phi,t)\bigr]\,\mathrm{d}t \;+\; g(t)\,\mathrm{d}\bar{w},
\label{eq:reverse_sde}\\
\frac{\mathrm{d}\phi}{\mathrm{d}t} &\;=\; f(\phi,t)-\tfrac{1}{2}\,g^{2}(t)\,s_{\theta}(\phi,t).
\label{eq:prob_flow_ode}
\end{align}
The training of $s_{\theta}$ is the subject of \Sec{sec:dm_score_matching}.

Although~\Eq{eq:reverse_sde_exact} and~\Eq{eq:prob_flow_ode_exact} have identical exact marginals, their discretisations with an imperfect $s_{\theta}$ behave very differently at the level of individual trajectories. The reverse SDE~\Eq{eq:reverse_sde} is stochastic and non-invertible, but its injected noise acts as a Langevin-like corrector that damps accumulated score errors at each step~\cite{Karras2022EDM}. The probability-flow ODE~\Eq{eq:prob_flow_ode} is deterministic and invertible, enabling likelihood evaluation via the continuous change-of-variables formula and latent-space interpolation. It also admits higher-order numerical solvers such as DPM-Solver~\cite{LuDPMSolver2022} or the Heun integrator of EDM~\cite{Karras2022EDM}, which reach high-quality generation in $10$--$35$ function evaluations.

The choice of sampler therefore involves a trade-off. A deterministic ODE integrator faithfully reproduces any systematic bias present in $s_{\theta}$, whereas the stochasticity of the reverse SDE provides a relaxation mechanism towards the true marginal at every time slice. For applications demanding high \emph{spectral fidelity}, in particular when the two-point function $G(p)$ of generated configurations is used as a physically meaningful quality metric (as in the propagator study of \Sec{sec:dm_propagator}), the reverse SDE, or a predictor-corrector hybrid that periodically injects Langevin noise into an ODE trajectory~\cite{Song2021SDE}, therefore tends to produce more faithful behaviour.

\subsection{Score matching}
\label{sec:dm_score_matching}

The reverse samplers~\Eq{eq:reverse_sde} and~\Eq{eq:prob_flow_ode} require the score $s(\phi,t)=\nabla_{\phi}\log p_{t}(\phi)$ at every noise level $t$. The marginal is itself an integral over the unknown data distribution,
\begin{equation}
p_{t}(\phi)\;=\;\int p_{\text{data}}(\phi_{0})\,p_{0t}(\phi\mid\phi_{0})\,\mathrm{d}\phi_{0},
\label{eq:marginal_pt}
\end{equation}
so $\nabla_{\phi}\log p_{t}$ is not available as a direct training target. \emph{Denoising score matching}~\cite{Vincent2011} sidesteps this by fitting $s_{\theta}(\phi,t)$ to the analytically known score of the \emph{conditional} distribution $p_{0t}(\phi_{t}\mid\phi_{0})$, minimising
\begin{multline}
\mathcal{L}(\theta) \;=\; \mathbb{E}_{t,\,\phi_{0},\,\phi_{t}\mid\phi_{0}}\Bigl[\lambda(t)\,\bigl\|s_{\theta}(\phi_{t},t)\\[-1pt]
-\nabla_{\phi_{t}}\log p_{0t}\bigl(\phi_{t}\,\bigl|\,\phi_{0}\bigr)\bigr\|^{2}\Bigr],
\label{eq:dsm}
\end{multline}
with $\lambda(t)>0$ a weighting function, $t\sim\mathrm{Uniform}(0,t_{\max})$, $\phi_{0}\sim p_{\text{data}}$, and $\phi_{t}\sim p_{0t}(\cdot\mid\phi_{0})$. A key identity~\cite{Vincent2011} shows that~\Eq{eq:dsm} and the (intractable) explicit score-matching loss $\mathbb{E}_{t,\phi_{t}}\!\bigl[\lambda(t)\|s_{\theta}(\phi_{t},t)-\nabla_{\phi_{t}}\log p_{t}(\phi_{t})\|^{2}\bigr]$ differ only by an additive $\theta$-independent constant. The global minimiser of~\Eq{eq:dsm} is therefore the \emph{marginal} score $\nabla_{\phi_{t}}\log p_{t}$, even though the target in~\Eq{eq:dsm} is only the conditional score. Because $p_{0t}(\phi_{t}\mid\phi_{0})$ is Gaussian with known mean and variance (\Eq{eq:vp_kernel} and~\Eq{eq:ve_sde_kernel}), this target is elementary and the objective is directly tractable. Equivalent reparameterisations predict the injected noise $\epsilon$ (DDPM's ``$\epsilon$-prediction''~\cite{Ho2020DDPM}) or the clean signal $\phi_{0}$.

For the VE schedule adopted in this work, the conditional score reads
\begin{equation}
\nabla_{\phi_{t}}\log p_{0t}\bigl(\phi_{t}\,\bigl|\,\phi_{0}\bigr) \;=\; -\frac{\phi_{t}-\phi_{0}}{\sigma^{2}(t)} \;=\; -\frac{\epsilon}{\sigma(t)},
\label{eq:ve_cond_score}
\end{equation}
having used the reparameterisation $\phi_{t}=\phi_{0}+\sigma(t)\,\epsilon$ with $\epsilon\sim\mathcal{N}(0,\mathbb{1})$. The standard weighting $\lambda(t)=\sigma^{2}(t)$ equalises the signal-to-noise ratio along the diffusion trajectory. Substituting~\Eq{eq:ve_cond_score} into~\Eq{eq:dsm} with this weighting reduces the objective to
\begin{equation}
\mathcal{L}(\theta) \;=\; \mathbb{E}_{t,\,\phi_{0},\,\epsilon}\!\left[\bigl\|\sigma(t)\,s_{\theta}(\phi_{t},t)+\epsilon\bigr\|^{2}\right],
\label{eq:dsm_loss_ve}
\end{equation}
the loss minimised in all subsequent training runs.

Since $p_{0}=p_{\text{data}}$ by construction, the marginal satisfies $p_{t}\to p_{\text{data}}$ as $t\to 0$, and a converged network satisfies
\begin{equation}
s_{\theta}(\phi,t)\;\xrightarrow[t\to 0]{}\;\nabla_{\phi}\log p_{\text{data}}(\phi)\;=\;-\,\nabla_{\phi}S[\phi],
\label{eq:score_small_t}
\end{equation}
where the final equality uses the Boltzmann form $p_{\text{data}}(\phi)\propto e^{-S[\phi]}$ of the target distribution. The small-$t$ limit of the learned score therefore reproduces the classical force $-\nabla_{\phi}S[\phi]$ of the action, as a byproduct of denoising-based training. The precision of this limit, and the sensitivity of the reverse samplers~\Eq{eq:reverse_sde} and~\Eq{eq:prob_flow_ode} to its residual error, underlie the diagnostics developed in \Sec{sec:acceptance} and \Sec{sec:score_quality}.


\section{Critical point of 2D \texorpdfstring{$\phi^4$}{phi4} theory from Wolff + Fourier-accelerated HMC}
\label{sec:phi4_critical}

We consider the scalar $\phi^4$ theory on a two-dimensional $L\times L$ square lattice with periodic boundary conditions. The lattice action reads
\begin{equation}
S[\phi] = \sum_{x} \Bigl[
  -2\kappa \sum_{\mu=1}^{2} \phi_x\,\phi_{x+\hat\mu}
  + \phi_x^2 + \lambda\bigl(\phi_x^2 - 1\bigr)^2
\Bigr],
\label{eq:phi4_action}
\end{equation}
where $\kappa$ is the hopping parameter and $\lambda$ the quartic self-coupling. In two dimensions we set $\lambda=0.022$. The model undergoes a $\mathbb{Z}_2$-symmetry-breaking phase transition at a critical value $\kappa_c(\lambda)$, which belongs to the 2D Ising universality class.

\subsection{Sampling algorithm}
\label{sec:wolff_fahmc}

Configurations are generated by a hybrid algorithm that alternates Fourier-accelerated Hybrid Monte Carlo (FA-HMC) updates with Wolff cluster flips.

\paragraph{FA-HMC.}
The standard HMC suffers from critical slowing down because the mass matrix in momentum space does not match the spectrum of the lattice operator. In Fourier-accelerated HMC, one introduces a momentum-dependent mass matrix in Fourier space,
\begin{equation}
M(\hat p) = \hat p^{\,2} + m_{\text{eff}}^2,\quad
\hat p^{\,2} = \sum_{\mu=1}^{D} 4\sin^2\!\Bigl(\frac{\pi k_\mu}{L}\Bigr),
\label{eq:fourier_mass}
\end{equation}
where $m_{\text{eff}}^2$ is estimated from the variance of Fourier modes during a warm-up phase and subsequently adapted in a series of windows. The conjugate momenta are sampled as $\pi\sim\mathcal{N}(0,M)$ and the kinetic energy is $K=\frac{1}{2}\pi^\top M^{-1}\pi$, both evaluated efficiently via FFT. The leapfrog integrator uses the velocity $v=M^{-1}\pi$ for the position update, ensuring that long-wavelength modes propagate with a step size comparable to that of short-wavelength modes. The step size $\varepsilon$ is tuned via dual averaging to maintain an acceptance rate of $\sim 75\%$.

\paragraph{Wolff cluster flip.}
After each FA-HMC trajectory, we perform $n_\text{Wolff}$ Wolff cluster updates that target the global $\mathbb{Z}_2$ mode. A cluster is grown from a random seed site by adding neighboring site $y$ to the cluster with bond probability
\begin{equation}
P_{\text{bond}}(x,y) = 1 - \exp\bigl(-4\kappa\,\phi_x\phi_y\bigr),
\label{eq:wolff_bond}
\end{equation}
whenever $\phi_x\phi_y > 0$. All sites in the cluster are then reflected: $\phi_x\to -\phi_x$. This step efficiently decorrelates the magnetization and drastically reduces critical slowing down for the order parameter.

\subsection{Observables}
\label{sec:observables}

From the spatially averaged magnetization per configuration, $\bar\phi = \frac{1}{V}\sum_x\phi_x$ with $V=L^2$, we measure:

\begin{enumerate}
\item \textbf{Order parameter:}
$\langle|\bar\phi|\rangle$.

\item \textbf{Magnetic susceptibility:}
$\chi = V\bigl(\langle|\bar\phi|^2\rangle - \langle|\bar\phi|\rangle^2\bigr)$.

\item \textbf{Binder cumulant:}
$U_L = 1 - \dfrac{\langle|\bar\phi|^4\rangle}{3\,\langle|\bar\phi|^2\rangle^2}$.
\end{enumerate}

\noindent
Statistical errors are estimated by the jackknife method.

\section{Momentum-space propagator from diffusion models}
\label{sec:dm_propagator}

Having established the critical points for the 2D and 3D $\phi^4$ theories via Wolff + FA-HMC, we now test whether a score-based diffusion model can faithfully reproduce the momentum-space propagator $G(p)$.
We train a noise-conditional score network (NCSN++) on HMC-generated configurations at $L=128$ and $\lambda=0.022$, then generate independent samples by reverse-diffusion and compare the radially-averaged propagator
\begin{equation}
G(|p|) = \bigl\langle |\tilde\phi(p)|^2 \bigr\rangle_{\text{radial}}
\label{eq:propagator}
\end{equation}
against the HMC reference, where $\tilde\phi(p)$ is the discrete Fourier transform of a configuration. Errors are estimated via bootstrap.

We consider three representative values of the hopping parameter that span the phase diagram:
\begin{itemize}
\item $\kappa = 0.26$, in the symmetric phase;
\item $\kappa = 0.2705 \approx \kappa_c$, near the critical point;
\item $\kappa = 0.28$, in the broken phase.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Acceptance rate as a training diagnostic}
\label{sec:acceptance}

A natural quantitative measure of how well a diffusion model has learned the target distribution is the Metropolis--Hastings (MH) acceptance rate of a Metropolis-Adjusted Langevin Algorithm (MALA)~\cite{Roberts1998} that uses the score network as its proposal drift.

In the reverse-diffusion framework, the score network $s_\theta(\phi,t)\approx\nabla_\phi\log p_t(\phi)$ is trained to approximate the score of the noised distribution $p_t$. At small diffusion time $t\to 0$, $p_t\to p_0\propto e^{-S}$ and therefore $s_\theta(\phi,t)\to -\nabla_\phi S(\phi)$. From the current state $\phi_\tau$, the MALA proposal is
\begin{equation}
\psi_{\tau+1} = \phi_\tau + h\,s_\theta(\phi_\tau,\,t_{\text{mh}}) + \sqrt{2h}\,\eta,
\label{eq:mala_proposal}
\end{equation}
where $h$ is the step size, $t_{\text{mh}}$ the diffusion time at which the score is evaluated, and $\eta\sim\mathcal{N}(0,\mathds{1})$. Conditioned on $\phi_\tau$, the proposal is a Gaussian with mean $\phi_\tau + h\,s_\theta(\phi_\tau,t_{\text{mh}})$ and variance $2h\,I_n$, so the proposal kernel has the explicit form
\begin{widetext}
\begin{equation}
q(\psi_{\tau+1}|\phi_\tau) = \frac{1}{(4\pi h)^{n/2}}\exp\!\Bigl(-\frac{1}{4h}\bigl\|\psi_{\tau+1} - \phi_\tau - h\,s_\theta(\phi_\tau,t_{\text{mh}})\bigr\|^2\Bigr),
\label{eq:mala_kernel}
\end{equation}
\end{widetext}
where $n=L^D$ is the number of degrees of freedom and $p(\phi)\propto e^{-S(\phi)}$ is the known physical distribution. The accept-reject step is
\begin{equation}
\phi_{\tau+1} =
\begin{cases}
\psi_{\tau+1} & \text{with prob. } \min\!\bigl\{1,\,\tfrac{p(\psi_{\tau+1})\,q(\phi_\tau|\psi_{\tau+1})}{p(\phi_\tau)\,q(\psi_{\tau+1}|\phi_\tau)}\bigr\}, \\[6pt]
\phi_\tau      & \text{otherwise}.
\end{cases}
\label{eq:mala_accept}
\end{equation}
The transition probability ratio can be computed explicitly as
\begin{widetext}
\begin{equation}
\frac{p(\psi_{\tau+1})\,q(\phi_\tau|\psi_{\tau+1})}{p(\phi_\tau)\,q(\psi_{\tau+1}|\phi_\tau)}
= \exp\!\Bigl(
  -S(\psi_{\tau+1}) - \tfrac{1}{4h}\bigl\|\phi_\tau - \psi_{\tau+1} - h\,s_\theta(\psi_{\tau+1},t_{\text{mh}})\bigr\|^2
 + S(\phi_\tau) + \tfrac{1}{4h}\bigl\|\psi_{\tau+1} - \phi_\tau - h\,s_\theta(\phi_\tau,t_{\text{mh}})\bigr\|^2
\Bigr).
\label{eq:mala_ratio}
\end{equation}
\end{widetext}
Substituting the proposal \eq{eq:mala_proposal}, i.e.\ $\psi_{\tau+1}-\phi_\tau - h\,s_\theta(\phi_\tau) = \sqrt{2h}\,\eta$, the log acceptance probability simplifies to
\begin{widetext}
\begin{equation}
\log\alpha = \bigl[S(\phi_\tau)-S(\psi_{\tau+1})\bigr]
+ \tfrac{1}{2}\|\eta\|^2
- \tfrac{1}{4h}\bigl\|\sqrt{2h}\,\eta + h\bigl[s_\theta(\phi_\tau,t_{\text{mh}})+s_\theta(\psi_{\tau+1},t_{\text{mh}})\bigr]\bigr\|^2,
\label{eq:mala_logalpha}
\end{equation}
\end{widetext}
which is the form used in practice. The MH step guarantees that, regardless of the quality of $s_\theta$, the chain targets the correct distribution $e^{-S}$. The acceptance rate $\langle\min(1,e^{\log\alpha})\rangle$ therefore measures how closely the score-network proposal matches the true Langevin drift $-\nabla_\phi S$: a well-trained model yields a high acceptance rate, while a poorly trained one is rejected more often.

\paragraph{Parameter calibration.}
Two parameters must be chosen: the step size $h$ and the diffusion time $t_{\text{mh}}$. The calibration criterion is the \emph{diagnostic power}, defined as the difference in acceptance rate between a late-epoch (well-trained) and an early-epoch (poorly trained) checkpoint.

\paragraph{Step-size scaling.}
For MALA on a $d$-dimensional i.i.d.\ product target $\pi(x)=\prod_i f(x_i)$, Roberts and Rosenthal~\cite{Roberts1998} proved that the optimal proposal variance scales as $h\sim d^{-1/3}$, yielding an optimal acceptance rate of $0.574$. For a $D$-dimensional lattice with $d=L^D$ sites, this would give $h\sim L^{-D/3}$, i.e.\ $h\sim L^{-2/3}$ in 2D and $h\sim L^{-1}$ in 3D. However, the $\phi^4$ action contains nearest-neighbour couplings ($-2\kappa\sum_{\langle x,y\rangle}\phi_x\phi_y$) that strongly violate the i.i.d.\ assumption. The total action change for a global proposal scales as $\Delta S \sim d\cdot h$, so maintaining $\Delta S\sim O(1)$ for reasonable acceptance requires the RWM-type scaling $h\sim 1/d = 1/L^D$ rather than the MALA-optimal $d^{-1/3}$. Empirically, $h\sim L^{-D/3}$ produces a step size that is far too large in both 2D and 3D. We therefore adopt $h=c/L^D$ throughout, i.e.\ $h=c/L^2$ in 2D and $h=c/L^3$ in 3D, with the coefficient $c$ calibrated independently for each dimensionality.

We find that the diagnostic power is maximised at $t_{\text{mh}}=10^{-4}$, which is much smaller than values typically used in sampling ($t\sim 10^{-2}$). As shown in \fig{fig:calibrate_tmh}, at large $t_{\text{mh}}\gtrsim 5\times10^{-3}$ the gap reverses sign: the score at large $t$ approximates the score of a heavily smeared distribution $p_t\neq p_0$, so a well-trained model's proposal is rejected more often by the exact action than a poorly trained one whose drift is weak and effectively random. This reversal makes large $t_{\text{mh}}$ unsuitable as a diagnostic. For the step size in 2D, \fig{fig:calibrate_h} shows that $c=0.2$ (i.e.\ $h=0.2/L^2$) maximises the diagnostic power while keeping the late-epoch acceptance rate in the informative range $50$--$80\%$.

\subsection{Direct score quality diagnostic}
\label{sec:score_quality}

While the MALA acceptance rate provides an effective indirect measure of score quality, it depends on the step size $h$ and evaluation time $t_{\text{mh}}$, requiring careful calibration (\fig{fig:calibrate_h}, \fig{fig:calibrate_tmh}).  A more direct diagnostic is to compare the model output $s_\theta(\phi,t)$ against the true gradient $-\nabla_\phi S(\phi)$ at a small diffusion time $t$, without any Metropolis--Hastings sampling.

For each reference configuration $\phi$ drawn from the training data, we evaluate the model score $s_\theta(\phi,t_{\text{eval}})$ at $t_{\text{eval}}=10^{-4}$ and compute the cosine similarity
\begin{equation}
C = \frac{\langle s_\theta,\, -\nabla S \rangle}{\|s_\theta\|\;\|\nabla S\|},
\label{eq:cos_sim}
\end{equation}
the relative mean-squared error $\|s_\theta - (-\nabla S)\|^2 / \|\nabla S\|^2$, and the magnitude ratio $\|s_\theta\|/\|\nabla S\|$, averaged over $N=512$ configurations. The choice $t_{\text{eval}}=10^{-4}$ (noise level $\sigma\approx 0.01$, or $0.5\%$ of the data range in normalised space) ensures that $p_t\approx p_0$ to high accuracy while the score estimate remains numerically stable. A larger evaluation time $t=0.01$ ($\sigma\approx 0.10$) introduces non-negligible Gaussian smoothing that systematically reduces the magnitude ratio below unity even for a perfectly trained model, making it a less informative diagnostic; a smaller time $t=10^{-5}$ ($\sigma\approx 0.003$) enters the regime where division by $\sigma(t)$ in the network output amplifies numerical noise.

\fig{fig:score_quality_2d} shows all three metrics as a function of training epoch for $L=128$ at all three $\kappa$ values. The cosine similarity rises from $\sim 0.99$ at epoch~49 to $\sim 0.998$ at late training; the relative MSE drops by more than an order of magnitude; and the magnitude ratio converges toward unity from above, starting at $\sim 1.5$ and reaching $\sim 0.9$--$1.0$ by epoch~5000. These trends are consistent with the monotonic improvement observed in the MALA acceptance rate (\fig{fig:acc_vs_epoch_2d}) and confirm that the score network progressively learns both the direction and the magnitude of $-\nabla S$.

\subsection{Quantitative propagator-mismatch metrics}
\label{sec:prop_mismatch_metrics}

To quantify how well the diffusion-model propagator $G_{\text{DM}}(k)$ matches the reference $G_{\text{train}}(k)$ across momentum bins, one could compare them via absolute deviation $\Delta G(k)=G_{\text{DM}}-G_{\text{train}}$ or relative deviation $\Delta G/G_{\text{train}}$, but neither scales uniformly across the UV/IR range. The absolute deviation is dominated by a handful of low-$k$ bins where $G$ is large, while the relative deviation grows artificially at high $k$ where $G$ is tiny and the mismatch contributes negligibly to the field variance. We instead use three complementary metrics, each with a sharp probabilistic or statistical interpretation.

\paragraph{(i) Per-mode Gaussian KL.}
Under translation invariance, any zero-mean Gaussian field measure diagonalises in momentum with covariance $G(k)$, and the modes become independent. The Kullback--Leibler divergence between the two Gaussian field distributions $p_{\text{train}}=\mathcal{N}(0,G_{\text{train}})$ and $p_{\text{DM}}=\mathcal{N}(0,G_{\text{DM}})$ therefore reduces to an additive sum over momentum modes:
\begin{equation}
D_{\text{KL}}(p_{\text{train}}\|p_{\text{DM}}) = \sum_k D_k,\qquad
D_k = \tfrac{1}{2}\bigl[r_k - 1 - \log r_k\bigr],\quad r_k=\tfrac{G_{\text{train}}(k)}{G_{\text{DM}}(k)}.
\label{eq:per_mode_kl}
\end{equation}
The function $f(r)=r-1-\log r$ vanishes at $r=1$, is strictly convex, grows linearly as $r\to\infty$ (linear penalty for DM underestimating the variance) and only logarithmically as $r\to 0^+$ (weaker penalty for overestimation). For small relative deviations $r=1+\varepsilon$ one has $D_k\simeq\varepsilon^2/4$, so $D_k$ reduces to the usual quadratic relative-deviation measure in the well-trained regime, but is far tamer than $(r-1)^2$ for large mismatches in the UV tail. Because $D_k$ is dimensionless and additive, contributions from different $|k|$-bins (and hence UV vs IR halves) can be compared and summed on equal footing.

Crucially, \eq{eq:per_mode_kl} is the KL between two \emph{Gaussian surrogates} determined by the two-point functions alone. When the underlying field theory is non-Gaussian (as in $\phi^4$ with interactions), the metric captures the second-moment mismatch but is blind to higher-cumulant structure such as the connected four-point function $\langle\phi^4\rangle_{\text{conn}}$; matching the two-point function is a necessary but not sufficient condition for distributional equivalence. The generalisation $D_{\text{KL}}(p\|q_{\text{Gauss}})=D_{\text{KL}}(p_{\text{Gauss}}\|q_{\text{Gauss}})+J(p)$, with $J(p)\ge 0$ the negentropy of the true distribution, makes the missing piece explicit.

\paragraph{(ii) Statistical $z$-score.}
Bootstrap-resampling the configurations yields uncertainty estimates $\sigma_{\text{DM}}(k)$ and $\sigma_{\text{train}}(k)$ on the two propagators. The standardised residual
\begin{equation}
z_k = \frac{G_{\text{DM}}(k)-G_{\text{train}}(k)}{\sqrt{\sigma_{\text{DM}}^2(k)+\sigma_{\text{train}}^2(k)}}
\label{eq:prop_zscore}
\end{equation}
tests whether the per-bin discrepancy is statistically significant given the finite sample size. Bins with $|z_k|\lesssim 2$ are consistent with the reference at the $2\sigma$ level; persistent deviations with $|z_k|\gg 2$ across many bins signal a real bias rather than sampling noise. The root-mean-square $z_{\text{rms}}=\sqrt{\langle z_k^2\rangle}$ provides a single scalar that saturates at $\mathcal{O}(1)$ when the mismatch is at the sample-noise floor and grows without bound when the bias dominates.

\paragraph{(iii) Phase-space weighted propagator difference.}
Expressed in the continuum limit, the field variance decomposes as $\langle\phi^2\rangle=\int\! d^Dk/(2\pi)^D\,G(k)\propto\int_0^\infty\! dk\,k^{D-1}G(k)$, so that each radial momentum shell contributes its propagator weighted by the phase-space measure $k^{D-1}$. Accordingly, the density
\begin{equation}
w_k = k^{D-1}\bigl[G_{\text{DM}}(k)-G_{\text{train}}(k)\bigr]
\label{eq:ps_weighted_delta}
\end{equation}
measures, up to an overall $(2\pi)^{-D}$ factor, how much each momentum shell contributes to the total mismatch in $\langle\phi^2\rangle$. Integrating $w_k$ over a UV or IR band directly quantifies the corresponding contribution to the two-point mismatch in position space, with physical units. Compared to $D_k$ and $z_k$, $w_k$ has a concrete physical interpretation but is not dimensionless, so the three metrics should be viewed as complementary rather than redundant.

\paragraph{Aggregate scalars.}
Each per-mode quantity above also admits a single-scalar summary. These scalars are convenient training diagnostics, one number per checkpoint.
\begin{align}
D_{\mathrm{tot}} &= \sum_k D_k,
  \label{eq:Dtot}\\
L_1^{\mathrm{pw}} &= \sum_k k^{D-1}\bigl|G_{\mathrm{DM}}(k)-G_{\mathrm{train}}(k)\bigr|,
  \label{eq:L1pw}\\
R_{\mathrm{var}} &= \frac{\sum_k G_{\mathrm{DM}}(k)}{\sum_k G_{\mathrm{train}}(k)}.
  \label{eq:Rvar}
\end{align}
$D_{\mathrm{tot}}$ is the Gaussian-surrogate KL of \Eq{eq:per_mode_kl} summed over bins, and inherits its ratio-based scale invariance. $L_1^{\mathrm{pw}}$ is the $L_1$ norm of the phase-space-weighted density $w_k$ of \Eq{eq:ps_weighted_delta}, and by Parseval's relation controls the absolute $\langle\phi^2\rangle$ mismatch in physical units. $R_{\mathrm{var}}$ is the total-variance ratio, tracked directly rather than via $|R_{\mathrm{var}}-1|$ because its signed deviation records whether the model underestimates or overestimates the bulk variance at each training stage. The three scalars carry complementary information. $D_{\mathrm{tot}}$ is insensitive to a uniform rescaling of $G$ that $R_{\mathrm{var}}$ catches immediately. $L_1^{\mathrm{pw}}$ weights mid-$k$ modes most strongly and is the closest of the three to a perceptual summary.

\paragraph{Perceptual benchmark: FID.}
For image data we cross-check the spectral diagnostics against the Fréchet Inception Distance (FID) \cite{Heusel2017FID}. Passing a sample set through the Inception-v3 network yields $d=2048$-dimensional activations; fitting a Gaussian $\mathcal{N}(\mu, \Sigma)$ to these activations for both the DM and training samples, FID is the Fréchet distance between the two Gaussians
\begin{equation}
\mathrm{FID} = \|\mu_{\mathrm{DM}}-\mu_{\mathrm{train}}\|^2
  + \mathrm{tr}\!\left[\Sigma_{\mathrm{DM}}+\Sigma_{\mathrm{train}}
    -2\bigl(\Sigma_{\mathrm{DM}}\Sigma_{\mathrm{train}}\bigr)^{1/2}\right],
\label{eq:fid}
\end{equation}
which, like $D_{\mathrm{tot}}$, is a Gaussian KL up to a monotone rescaling but in perceptual Inception-feature space rather than in momentum space. Comparing FID across training with the spectral scalars in \Eqs{eq:Dtot}{eq:Rvar} tests how much perceptual and physical-spectrum quality track each other.

\paragraph{Illustration on CelebA $64\times64$.}
\fig{fig:diag_celeba64} applies these three diagnostics to a NCSN++ diffusion model trained on CelebA grayscale $64\times64$ images, using log-spaced checkpoints from epoch~1 to epoch~$10{,}000$. Each checkpoint supplies $N=512$ EM-sampled images; the reference $G_{\text{train}}$ is computed from $50{,}000$ training images, so $\sigma_{\text{train}}\ll\sigma_{\text{DM}}$ and the DM sample size dominates the bootstrap uncertainty. All three metrics display the expected monotonic improvement during training: the per-mode KL drops by nearly two orders of magnitude at all $|k|$, the $z$-score envelope contracts toward the $\pm 2\sigma$ band, and the phase-space weighted difference $w_k$ shrinks and changes sign as the model passes from underestimating to slightly overestimating the low-$k$ power. The UV (large-$|k|$) portion dominates the total KL throughout training, consistent with the difficulty of learning high-frequency image modes.
